Designers Who Code, Coders Who Design

When I was first starting out there was one position for someone who built things on the Web: Webmaster. The webmaster designed and developed the website. Now we have front end and back end engineers, devOps, IS/IT, and designers.

I suppose I understand the disconnect between the front end and the back end. What I don't understand is separating design from front end development. I think the fact that there is often friction in the relationship of designers and front end engineers should tell us that those positions are not best separated.

To me, a front end developer should be able to design and a designer should be able to develop front end code--HTML, CSS, and a low-level knowledge of JavaScript.

In my experience a lot of friction happens between design and development because neither understands what the other is saying. I suppose one is predominantly right brained and the other left brained, but there doesn't seem to be any reason for them not to be able to speak the same language. Front end developers have often complained about the pickiness of designers and how things can't always be pixel perfect. Designers often complain about the limitations of the code and how their designs are changed or not accepted altogether.

If a designer knows the limitations of the code or how a web page or application is built then he or she could understand how their design would be implemented before he/she begins the design.

Likewise, if a developer understands how important the right colors, size, hierarchy, spacing, etc. are for communicating to the user then he or she could develop better code and understand why it's important to stick closely to the visual design.

My suggestion is that designers branch out and learn front end code (HTML, CSS, and JavaScript) and front end developers learn the basic concepts of design (spacing, hierarchy, typography, etc). Even if we have separation of our actual duties, being able to speak each other's languages could help to make better design and code choices.

Getting Older in Tech

I'm over 40 and have been learning web design and development for about 20 years. The Web itself is only 30 years old. I won't say that I've been anything special in the field, just an early adopter. But that early adoption means that I've gained a lot of experience handling the core languages and seen a lot of the different trends along the way.

But I'm old. At least in the eyes of many out there. Especially hiring managers.

As we go along we'll find that more and more developers and designers are going to be "old" in the coming decade. That leads to a lot of questions from startups and among the community of developers who still want to do amazing, cutting edge work. Here are the things that seem to be the most troublesome:

  • Older developers' bodies are slowing down. The reality is that as you get older so does your body. That includes your brain. I was speaking with another programmer about this. He's learning a lot of new languages at the same time. He believes that his younger self would have picked it up more quickly than his middle-aged self. As you get older you just need more time to process things. The upside is that you have become a more efficient learner. When you're young you use your energy to push through your mistakes (and you make a lot of mistakes because of your inexperience). However, the older you are the fewer mistakes you make because you know which paths to go down and which to avoid. This creates an efficiency that allows you to still compete.
  • Older developers have families and responsibilities. Most people are waiting until their late 20's (sometimes longer) to start a family. This means that a lot of the competition for jobs in this industry are going to young people with no attachments and few responsibilities. There is also a tendency to move from their homes to places like Silicon Valley or Austin to seek out good jobs in tech. It's not really a surprise that companies are places where "fitting in" is a really big deal. This is where a lot of your relationships will be formed. For older developers who already have families, the bean bag and ping pong workplace just isn't that important because we have people and responsibilities at home. This is just what happens as you get older. There are always corporations who are ready to give those older developers a good, steady job that allows them to support their families and be able to prioritize those responsiblities. The drawback is that the work you do is pretty safe and conservative. Few corporations are pushing the limits with what they're doing. That trade-off can be too much for some developers and they seek the startup/agency life which puts them back into the companies with bean bags and energy drink induced all night hackathons. It's just a weird place to be as an older developer because the market hasn't created a space that is vibrant in the kinds of projects you work on and grown up in employment.
  • Older developers have wisdom and experience. The one thing that continues to grow as you age, even as things slow down, is your wisdom and experience. Older developers and programmers should be thought of like bonds. Keep them around to age and mature and it will pay dividends in the future. When you've worked in a field for 20 years or more you've seen just about all there is. When the field is as old as the people working in it then they've literally seen everything. Why wouldn't a company want that sort of experience on their team? One issue is money. That experience and wisdom is worth something. Maybe worth more than companies are willing to spend. This could certainly be another hurdle for a seasoned developer in working with startups and small agencies. I would hope that companies and agencies begin to see that one older developer could be worth two younger, less skilled developers. Or that bringing in one veteran developer to a group of young developers (assuming the older developer is concerned with teaching and guiding) could actual grow the team faster in terms of their skills and outputs. This doesn't necessarily have to be a senior role but could be. Depends on the developer.

I'm sure there are a lot more issues. If you know of some leave them as a comment on the Twitter card below.

HTML-Only FAQs

Here's an accessible, easy-to-implement FAQ pattern that doesn't use JavaScript. It's one of my favorites.

See the Pen the HTML5 Details Tag for FAQs by Brian Haferkamp (@brianhaferkamp) CodePen.

The key to this FAQ is the <details> tag in HTML 5.2. There are a couple of notable parts to this. First, the FAQ group should go into the <details> element. That's essentially your wrapper. Inside that wrapper element there is a <summary> element. That is where you put the text or icon that the user clicks. In our case it is the question for the FAQ. The final element in the group is the content that is hidden and shows on click. This can be anything you can put into HTML (text, video, audio, images, etc.) so go crazy with it. Note that anything in the <details> wrapper that is after the <summary> element will be hidden until <summary> is clicked. This allows for multiple paragraphs or elments.

It's also possible to do some basic animations using CSS. I've put those in the example, too, just to show some of the things that are possible.

A Framework for Critiquing Web Design

Today I want to share some of my criteria for critiquing website designs. This does not have to do with code or interactions but the visual design itself. As most projects begin with some sort of visual design or elements, it's a good checklist to have.

Using words instead of visuals

Are you using words to say something that could be said with a visual? If so, then consider dropping the words and replacing the text with something visual, like a video, photo, infographic, or icon. According to this Fast Company article on visual content and memory, "visuals are processed 60,000 times faster than text."

Readability

Can the text be read easily? I'm looking at things like line length (how many words per line) and legibility (can you read the text over background colors or images) This is a little different than contrast. You want to make sure the text and buttons pop off of backgrounds. If the background is busy, consider a blur or darker color overlay.

Fonts

The right font can make or break a design. There is a reason that we have so many fonts to choose from. The main thing I want to know about the font is whether or not it's the right choice for the site/app. You know this by figuring out the target market for the product. For instance, Black letter or a Gothic script font is probably not the right choice for a mommy blog. The more mainstream your product or service, the more you will need to choose a rather bland font. Something strong and sans-serif works well when your audience is diverse.

The other thing I look at are font combinations. Do they work together? Are they both speaking the same thing to the same audience? If you're not confident in font pairings, consider looking to a site like typewolf.com, where you can learn all about fonts, pairings, and recommendations. Don't have the eye for font pairings, stick with one font family and work with different sizes and weights from that one family. It brings a sense of cohesion to the design when you do that. Check out this a step-by-step guide for choosing fonts for the web from Google.

You might also consider a super family that might have both serif and sans-serif fonts. Here are some examples of super families on Google Fonts that you can easily incorporate into a website.

Colors

The last major category is color. Things I'm looking at are appropriateness to the product and target audience and contrast/legibility.

There is actually a lot in the concept of appropriateness. If your product is only going to be consumed by the people of one country you need to know what those people grow up thinking about certain colors. Red in one country might not mean the same thing in other countries. One good example is seeing a person's name in red ink in Korea means that the person is deceased or (if living) that you wish them dead. That's probably not what you're going for in your design so if the audience is global, make sure you're not offending with the color choices you're making.

Another major consideration is contrast. Do the colors you choose (especially for text) have enough contrast for the Web? In print, there are some things you can get away with, but when you build things online you have no control of the output for each user. Therefore, you need to follow some standard design rules. To check whether or not your colors have enough contrast you can use a tool like the color contrast checker from WebAIM. It allows you to input your foreground and background colors and check the contrast levels. Passing the AA level (4.5:1) is a good start. If you can pass on the AAA level (7:1) then you're doing great. You might find that this cramps your design aesthetic, but you're probably just going to have to change the way you think between the Web and print. It's not the same thing.

Hopefully, this gives you a nice framework for critiquing website designs. The Web is not print. We have no control of the outputs where our work will be shown. So use the tools referenced above and you'll start to understand why websites look the way they do and begin to work within those boundaries to create amazing designs of your own.

My Everyday Web Development Tools

As web developers, we use a lot of tools. Most people with a craft do. So here are some of my favorite tools to use.

Adobe XD

My work team has adopted Adobe XD for all of our design work. I love its simplicity and that we can create interactive mockups that can be shared on the Web. Animations, vectors, and more make this program an amazing addition. Photoshop and Illustrator work well, but they aren't necessarily made for what we do. XD hits all the right notes for a web developer.

FastStone Image Viewer

This little Windows program is an essential part of my image editing workflow. When Lightroom or Photoshop are too heavy I turn to FastStone Image Viewer to do light editing. I also use the screenshot tool all the time to grab images of whole windows or specific areas. It opens quickly and does all the little jobs perfectly.

Atom

I've been using Atom as my code editor for years. I love that it's open source, configurable, and really lightweight. It's got all the basics plus people write plugins for specific libraries and languages. It also connects very easily to Github so you can push and pull right from Atom. Slick.

My Typical Stack

About half of our projects at work are WordPress and the other half use my typical stack: PugJS, SASS, jQuery, BrowserSync, Gulp. It's a node-based workflow that I've added to over the years to get up and running quickly. It includes a material design icon font.

I call my build system, The 20-Mile Head Start, because if building a site were a marathon, this system is like being driven to mile 20 and then starting the race. It saves so much time in setup and all you need to get going is node.js.

WordPress

When I reach for a CMS it's always WordPress. The community is so large, the resources plentiful, and PHP is easy to work with. It's a no-brainer. That said, I try to steer our projects away from a CMS unless completely necessary. There is some maintenance work that must go into WordPress so building with it means maintaining it for the long haul. That's something I try to keep in mind in recommending a stack for each project.

Webflow

I've just started to get into Webflow over the past year. It's not what I thought it was--in a good way. I thought it would be a Wix-type editor, but that's not the concept. Webflow is a visual text editor. That's the best way to describe it. There aren't a lot of prebuilt components out of the box that you simply drag and drop into a site. Rather, it is a visual way to write code. If you take that thought into it I think it will make more sense. It also makes it enjoyable for web developers to use who already know how to code. This is no little sweet cupcake of a website; it is a full development program. Kudos to the Webflow team.

FYI, the ease of creating relatively sophisticated JavaScript interactions has almost made me switch to it exclusively.

That's certainly not all of the tools in my toolbox. I've left out lots of good ones (for instance, jslint.com and these material design shadows on Codepen). But these are the main tools I use on a daily basis. If they might be helpful to you, check them out and give them a try.

Why is all of this important? It is a user-centered approach. Serving one image for all screens is not ideal because small devices are usually on data networks or Wi-Fi, which are inherently slower than Ethernet connections. Small devices also have less processing power than desktop or laptop computers. This all means users experience slower loading times and use more bandwidth (i.e., data) for no particular reason.

A really cool advantage of this technique is you get to direct how users see images at certain screen sizes. If you serve one image that looks great on a large desktop it might not look as good on a mobile phone. Some of the details will be lost because the image is shrinking to a smaller overall size. Learn the Basics

I consider myself lucky to have learned web development when I did in the late 1990s. It was a real wild west so you could make things that weren't that sophisticated and you were considered a success. Drop down menus and app-like UIs were pretty crazy. A little CSS could make your site really stand out.

For people going into web development today, the lines are not so straight. It seems like there are a lot of short cuts to getting to web development. Lots of tools that will help make your life easier—if you know what you're doing already. That's the tricky part, right? Getting to competency.

My suggestion to new web developers is to get very proficient at building products on the Web using the baseline languages—HTML, CSS, and JavaScript. Full Height Content Section Using CSS Grid

Have you ever had a short piece of content sandwiched between a header and footer that just doesn't take up enough space to force the footer all the way to the bottom of the browser window? This happens to me all the time on redirect pages after form submissions. Here's how it looks:

What we want to do is push the footer to the bottom. We could use fixed positioning:

footer {position: fixed; bottom: 0; width: 100%;}

This is the look that we are going for but doing this takes the footer out of the flow of the document and could mess things up for us later on. Here's how it looks:

We could also try: 

footer {min-height: 100vh;}

That does the trick, but it's a little brute force for my taste. It also pushes the footer past the bottom of the browser's bottom edge. This is what it looks like:

Okay, now let's get to a better solution. I've found that the best way to do this is to use CSS Grid Layout. First, we apply Grid to our body element:

body {display: grid; grid-template-rows: auto 1fr auto;}

This code alone doesn't do anything, but it gives us the framework for the magic to happen. To make the content section take up all the available space, we need to add in a minimum height for the grid we just made. This will make it stretch from the top of the viewport to the bottom of the viewport even if there is not enough content. Here's the code with the min-height property applied to the grid:

body {display: grid; grid-template-rows: auto 1fr auto; min-height: 100vh;}

Notice that we are explicitly giving values to the three rows of our grid. The first and last are auto which means the elements will take up whatever space they require. The middle value represents our content section. That should take up all of the remaining space. We do that with the 1fr value. Voila! You have a relatively positioned grid that takes up all of the viewport's height at the very least. It will also continue to allow the main content section to grow if you put a lot of content in there and it pushes everything down. The minimum height is 100vh, so it will continue to grow as content pushes the elements down the page past the bottom of the browser window.

To center everything, just add this to the main content section:

#content {place-self: center;}

We now have center-aligned (vertically and horizontally) content inside a main content section that pushes our footer to the bottom of the browser window and doesn't mess up the flow of the document to do it.

The Developer's New Groove

Tips on finding your own groove so you can write better code and enjoy doing it

Getting motivated to write code can sometimes be a chore. For me, the hard parts are at the beginning of projects I don't want to do or the middle of projects I've already started. When I have something I'm excited to start working on it's easy to take off and start coding. Getting a project through the middle stages, however, is more difficult for me. One thing that has really helped over the years is developing a work groove. I just made that up. There must be a technical name for that. What I mean is that I sit in the same chair, have a routine, and even have "work" music. If I want to get stuff done I have playlists that help me focus in on writing code. The playlists go in cycles for me, but usually it's metal and hard rock. Here are my Rock Favorites and Blindside, one of my favorite Swedish metal bands of all time. Sometimes Twenty One Pilots or Switchfoot does the trick, too. It's as though my brain knows when certain music comes on that it's time to write code.

Here are some things you can do to find your own groove.

  • Set a consistent place to work. It might take working in a couple of spots to find your ideal work environment. Once you find it, do it every time. I sit in the same chair on the same side of the table each day. My desk stays arranged the same way at the office, too.
  • Set a consistent time to work. It's important to have your body know it's work time. Train it by working a consistent schedule. This is easier for company work and harder if you're a freelancer or work from home. On my work from home days I try to keep the same schedule as my office days. If you're a remote worker identify your rhythms. Are you most alert in the morning, middle of the day, or at night. If you can adjust your coding time to these periods of the day your time in the code might be more productive and enjoyable.

Then shower and either get ready to drive to the office or sit down to code. Last thing I do before I turn on the computer is get a cup of coffee.   • Find out what blocks out distractions for you. For me, it's the right music. Static/white noise, traffic, looking at a particular coffee shop mural, silence. Whatever you need around you to get yourself focused, find it and do it.

In the beginning it will take some time to figure these things out for yourself. Try something. If it doesn't work try something else. Make note of when you're most productive.

Web development is a job full of repetition and routine. Becoming a creature of habit will help your brain and body get into the groove.

Accessibility is Part of the Job

Accessibility has been a concern for users of the Web since the beginning. I've read the statistic that 1 in 5 users of the Web need some sort of assistance when using a browser. That could be as severe as complete blindness all the way to just needing to make the text larger for older eyes. For the beginner dev all the way to the seasoned professional accessibility should be a chief concern for a front end developer. It is one of the things that a Web developer specifically brings to the table every time.

I want to share some baseline thoughts and resources to help you create better (i.e., more accessible) sites, pages, and apps.

Here are some practical first steps, or basics, of Web accessibility:

  1. Write semantic markup. One of the best ways to get started writing more accessible pages is to use appropriate HTML5 markup. Don't use a div when there is a more appropriate semantic tag available. Look over your naked code in HTML (without CSS) and see if there are areas that you can clearly markup as headers, footers, navigation, and so on. Is there a more sensible way to separate the content? Use some (or all) of the resources at the end of this post to see or hear how the site will be accessed through the HTML.
  2. Allow your visual content to speak. Visual content is great for sighted users. However, what if images are turned off or the user is unable to see them? There are rock solid ways to let the user know the content of images on the Web. Note that if the image is described in the content itself, or is only there for decoration, no alt text is necessary. Go through your images and determine what visual content needs to be understood and what is on the page simply to make things look good.
  3. Make forms easier to understand and use. This is one of the big ones. Forms have been the bane of existence from the beginning but they are very important to the modern Web so you need to know how to use them and use them well. The biggest thing you can do is connect the form label with the input it describes. (And yes, you need to use the label tag for your inputs.) Using a label for your inputs allows the user to tab understandably and will also help screen readers connect labels with the appropriate inputs in a way that can be audibly understood.
  4. Use the right tags for links. Links are another big issue. Use an anchor link when the link takes you to another page or off the site completely (an HTTP request) and use a button when the action happens on the same page or view. You get some inherent styling and behavior when you use the right elements. This is one of those instances. If you try to do everything through JavaScript then you're essentially building the behavior back into the element that you could have gotten by just using the right element in the first place. Do the right thing.
  5. Give interactive elements some space. This is one that not many people think about. Modern design (think Apple.com) is built on the premise of large type and lots of nice negative (white) space between sections, words, and elements. This is more than a design aesthetic, it's also a practical way to give users with disabilities the opportunity to take the right actions. If the clickable elements are too close together then the odds of clicking the wrong thing go up. Can you click the right element when your hands are shaking? Then there is probably enough space between them. If not, create some space with margins and make the hit areas larger through padding.

That's only scratching the surface. I promise you, though, that adding these elements in will not only help disabled users but the site or app in general. The bots will also love you and understand your content better which will help boost your search rankings. Devs who come along after you will probably praise you.

Here are some great resources to get you started:

  • Accessibility on Mozilla Developer Network (MDN)
  • The A11Y Project
  • Web Accessibility Evaluation Tool (WAVE)

Every small step you take helps take care of the ecosystem of a site or app. It's like picking up a piece of trash on the ground next to a trash can and throwing it away. It seems small but it's the these small (but easy) things that add up over time. Take the time to learn accessibility well and then do it.

You will need the skills of a researcher and the persistence of a beat reporter to do this work. There is a lot of information out there. Learn how to start with what you know and then begin synthesizing information and keywords into a better search.

In some ways I envy the devs starting out today. However, starting out in the Wild West of web development meant that I had to deconstruct what others had done and consume the few books available. This made me a better developer and gave me the skills that I still use each day I come to work. I hope that your journey goes well. Get stuck. Break things so you can fix them. Be persistent. Ask questions. These are the keys to learning how to build things on the web.

So I started working with jQuery almost exclusively (although now I know how to convert things back and forth to vanilla JS, when necessary).

For years now people have tried to get away from jQuery because it's an extra 30kb download (30kb...for shame!). I mean, seriously, the average web page is over 2mb. I don't really want to hear talk about an extra 30kb.

For me, jQuery represents a bridge to JavaScript, much like Algebra is a bridge to higher level mathematics for many. jQuery uses real words. It shortens things up so you can just use a method to do things. It makes my code more readable. Essentially, it's designer speak not developer speak.

I'm sticking with jQuery.

Listening for Appropriate Solutions

One of the struggles of any profession is providing appropriate solutions to problems. The old adage is, "To a hammer, everything looks like a nail." Web developers are no different. For the JavaScript specialist, everything can be fixed with JS. For the PHP developer, everything can be solved with databases and PHP. But what if the needs of the client are not conducive to your primary skill set? This is where the challenge comes in. Do you continue forward knowing that what you're going to build is a lot more complicated than the client needs? Or will you convey to the client they need something else then refer them to someone who can more appropriately handle his/her needs? Maybe you choose a third way and build what is appropriate but with secondary or tertiary skills. That's an option, too.

One way to help clients is to listen to them and ask them questions that help determine an appropriate path. Do they just need something small and lightweight that won't be changing anytime soon? Perhaps a standard HTML landing page is the best way to go. Do they need a little more but not quite a full-blown system that needs to be managed? Look into a larger HTML site but using something like Webflow to manage it. Does the client think he/she needs a small site when he/she really needs a fully managed CMS? Then explain how a management system can make the client's life easier. You are the consultant. You are fluent in Web things. Your expertise matters here at the front end of the project.

The problem with doing everything one way for all clients is that you put the client in a bad position. In doing this the developer is not looking out for the client. Instead, he/she is pushing his/her own system onto the client and ignoring the consequences that the client will have to live with. This started happening several years ago when devs began building so many sites with WordPress. The reality is that most clients don't need that level of sophistication. They just need a brochure site. WordPress is a system, though. A great system for creating dynamic content, but not a great system for creating sites that should just be static HTML. So it was an inappropriate build for many clients, but it was done because there were devs who could build the sites that way and clients who were being educated poorly about what they needed.

So here's a solution. Listen to the client. Determine the most appropriate development solution/stack/platform and then either deliver that out of your own knowledge or begin to create a network of other devs/agencies that you can pass clients along to who specialize in the work the client needs. Consider setting up a small finders fee, if the person/agency is willing. It would be better to make a client happy because you listened to him/her and provided an appropriate solution. Burdening the client with the responsibilities of a printing press when all he/she needed was a printer is irresponsible and short-sighted. Let's do better.

What is a Website?

Thoughts on understanding the value of a website

I sometimes have freelance clients who want me to build a website for them. Often, they are small businesses or solopreneurs. Many of them have no idea about websites or apps or what goes into building them. After the conversation they are surprised by the amount of work (and money) it costs to build a website. I often don't hear back from them. No negotiation. No questions.

I wonder sometimes if it would be helpful for them to understand what a website is. That way they could see the actual value of the website and it would be easier to attach a proper monetary value on the building of it.

So what is a website? These are the first 3 things I can think of:

  1. It is a platform in virtual space. Just like we build offices in the real world to handle business, provide work environments for employees, and to communicate with clients, a website does that in a virtual space. It says, "We exist. This is where you can find us."
  2. It is your chief virtual marketer. Almost all businesses need some form of marketing/sales. A website is a virtual marketer or salesperson that works 24/7, dresses how you want, and only says what you want it to say. Its reach is global. It can speak multiple languages. It never sleeps and never gets weary. It doesn't need a lunch break. This is a valuable employee.
  3. It is a portal for continuous two-way communication. Direct mail, advertising, email marketing--these are all one-way communication to prospective clients/customers. A website can provide two-way communication via email forms or live chat. It is a platform to schedule consultations and meetings. What it also does is give prospects a way to proactively reach out to you (giving permission), thereby empowering the client/customer. This is a great feeling in our modern age and lets prospects see you in a new light.

Based just on those three things, what would a client pay to have that available to him/her? What is the value of all those things? What is the value of a new office space? What is the value of a marketer or salesperson who never sleeps? What is the value of a two-way portal to clients? $100? $1000? $100,000? These are the questions that clients need to be confronted with.

I'm wondering if I need to have the value talk first thing in the conversation just to lay the groundwork for the value that is being provided to the client. Then we can get into the nitty gritty of what will be on the site itself. Maybe that would change the whole conversation about monetary value.

A Cog in the White Collar Assembly Line

May 8, 2019

Today, I read an interesting article called, The McDonaldization of UX. The author writes about the standardization of UX design team practices across major corporations and how that has turned him into a "wireframing monkey." There is no longer any creativity or risk. He is merely a cog in a white collar assembly line.

I'm a front end developer in a corporate marketing department and often feel the same way as the author of the article above. I receive designs that largely look like the last ones (for the sake of consistency) that need to go out in 1-4 week windows. In many ways, I feel like an assembly line piece, too. This has been a source of consternation for me over the past few years. It's a job I've wanted for much of my adult life. The benefits are good. The pay is okay. The company cares about the employees. There is also some security being in a 500-year-old industry.

How did it get this way, though? For many of the same reasons the article states: Time pressures (time to market, marketing deadlines, launch dates), efficiency (forced by downward pressures in the market), lack of understanding of the process of design and development (seeing us as service providers only and not as people who can bring any strategy to whole process), and product diversification (instead of continuously working on one solid product, creating many sub-branded offshoots of one bestseller). In a world where digital matters and there is so much downward pressure, corners must be cut to keep up. So we create more efficiency, use more frameworks, create more design systems, etc. to keep up. Be creative once and then repeat it—a lot.

However, one of the joys of web development is continually learning new things and producing a level of craft that matters. Sadly, corporate web development is based on as close to zero risk as you can get. Corporations make money by pumping out the same things over and over again. So I've become a code monkey of sorts.

How does one get out of this rut? Seems to me there are three good options:

  1. Quit and join a startup or small agency. If you do this just remember that most startups are trying to get to the level of the company you just left. All that freedom and creativity will go away as growth happens. It's not often that web developers are asked to continuously push forward in mature companies.
  2. Take on side work. Ah, the old side hustle. Start advertising yourself as a freelancer. The beautiful part is that you only have to take on work that you would like to do with clients you would like to work with. Keep your day job and let that be your initial investor. One key here is to position yourself well so that the types of clients you want to work with come knocking. If you're working corporate and you get 3-5 of these side jobs a year it might give you just the kind of risk and outlet you need for your creativity.
  3. Build your own ideas. A third option is to build out your own ideas. Do something with that great idea that you wish someone would make. Your job doesn't want to take your suggestion on a new project? Build out a prototype to get their attention. That's the route that I've taken with my work. I inject time to prototype new ideas and initiatives. I show them to those who are higher than me. I share them with the designers in the company. I've even created an online team to share innovation ideas across the company. Most of these will probably not turn into anything, but it at least gives me an outlet to ideate, prototype, and share with a likeminded community within the company.

I have to believe there is a way to have my cake and eat it, too. My day job is "code monkey." What I want to do and how I want to affect the world is so much bigger, though. So I've tried to create some spaces where I can utilize all of the new things I'm learning, even if my job has no use for them. If you've outgrown your job it might not be that you need to leave the regular paycheck. It might be that you just need to build some places for your new, expansive self to go within the confines of your present life.

Art Direction on the Web

May 7, 2019

I've been exploring the idea of art directed websites and web apps. This just means that you're doing something more than just stacking elements on top of each other. You're using the classic principles of design in a way that other media have been using them for the past 100 or so years. Magazines, books, movies—all of these are being art directed by someone.

Art direction is someone having a vision for communicating ideas in a certain way. There is a way to art direct a movie, photo shoot, book, or magazine. They aren't exactly the same, but the same principles are involved. In the end, it's about communicating an idea or feeling or thought to the viewer/reader through the design itself, not just the content.

For most of its existence, the Web has been focused on the technical aspects of communicating information to users. Can we get it to do what we want? That's been a big question. I think this is where we got design systems and frameworks. They can quickly scale up a site or app that works and doesn't look horrid.

That's been fine and good for a long time. However, new advances in the tools of the presentation layer, such as CSS Grid Layout and Flexbox, are opening up new possibilities. When we figure out that there is more room to stretch out and play—and the users are also expecting there to be more polished visual communication—then we should start to see that show up on the sites that we're building.

As a front end developer I've had the privilege to watch this change happen over the past 20 years. The things that I've dreamed about since I was young are actually possible now. Visions and what-ifs that I had many years ago are what I'm designing and creating today. It's awesome.

But now is the work. Now is the time to take those dreams and begin to build them. Now is the time to look at the amazing designs on Behance and Dribbble and begin to build them on the Web. Now is the time to learn CSS Grid and begin to play with it to see what it will do.

I hope that we can gradually move out of this rut of stacking blocks on top of one another and instead think more like Mondrian. He painted blocks, but the blocks became interesting and beautiful and speak to something far beyond just a bunch of blocks on top of one another.
